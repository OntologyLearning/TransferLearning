Workshop on Computational Topology and Data Analysis

Computational topology has played a synergistic role in bringing together research work from computational geometry, algebraic topology, data analysis, and many other related scientific areas. In recent years, the field has undergone particular growth in the area of data analysis. The application of topological techniques to traditional data analysis, which before has mostly developed on a statistical setting, has opened up new opportunities. This workshop is intended to cover this aspect of computational topology along with the developments of generic techniques for various topology-centered problems.

 

Besides people working in the field of computational topology, the workshop is targeted at researchers from computational geometry, data analysis, machine learning, and statistics, who want to learn about recent developments in the area.

Abstracts

 
Persistent cohomology, circular coordinates, and dynamical systems
Vin de Silva, Pomona College

Abstract: Persistent cohomology can be used to find circle-valued coordinate functions that reveal some of the topology carried by a data set. I will describe applications of this method to the study of periodic, quasi-periodic, and chaotic dynamical systems. In various parts, this is joint work with Mikael Vejdemo-Johansson, Dmitriy Morozov, Primoz Skraba, and Konstantin Mischaikow.
 
 
Persistent Homology of Time-Delay Embeddings
Jose Perea, Duke University

Abstract: We present in this talk a theoretical framework for studying the persistent homology of point clouds from time-delay (or sliding window) embeddings (f(t), f(t+τ), ..., f(t + Mτ)). Here f : R/2πZ → R, M ∈ N, τ > 0 and t ∈ R. In particular, we propose maximum persistence as a measure of periodicity at the signal level, f, present structural theorems for the resulting diagrams, and derive estimates for their dependency on the window size Mτ and embedding dimension M+1. We apply this methodology to quantifying periodicity in synthetic signals, and present comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in gene expression analysis. This is joint work with John Harer at Duke University, Mathematics Department.

 

 
Technicolor Topology: topological data analysis of typological color semantics
Mikael Vejdemo Johansson, KTH Royal Institute of Technology

 

Abstract: The World Color Survey (Kay, Berlin, Maffi, Merrifield, and Cook, 2009) is a massive data collection effort from typological linguistics, aiming at shedding light on the use of color words and the semantics and cognition of color perception in the world's languages. Primary color terms have been elicited from speakers of 110 languages, and compiled into a publicly available database. Machine learning and data analysis techniques have started making inroads into this area of linguistics research; with PCA analyses of the WCS data at the forefront of the research frontier (Jäger, Using Statistics for Cross-linguistic Semantics: A Quantitative Investigation of the Typology of Colour Naming Systems, 2012). We will be demonstrating and discussing how topological tools can aid and automate analysis tasks in a data-driven approach to color semantics; how the shape of the spoken color space can be extracted with various techniques; and how topology provides an approach to automate the construction of semantic maps of color terms.
 
 
Efficient Data Structure and Filtering Strategies for Computing Persistent Cohomology
Clement Maria, INRIA Sophia Antipolis
 
Abstract: Algorithms to compute the persistent homology of a simplicial complex in a field F consist of reducing an incidence matrix with F coefficients, in order to compute a pairing of the simplices corresponding to the creation and destruction of homology features. We propose an implementation of the recently introduced algorithm for persistent cohomology, which maintains isomorphic structures to homology because of duality, and we take advantage of the structure cohomology provides. We separate the representation of the simplicial complex from the representation of the (co)homology groups, and introduce a new data structure for maintaining the matrix, which is more compact and reduces substantially the amount of matrix operations in F. Building on the knowledge of both combinatorial structure of the complex and topological information provided by the data structures, we propose on-the-fly reordering heuristics of simplices along the computation which simplify the representation of the (co)homology groups and improve both time and memory complexities. Our implementation performance depends mostly on quantities attached to the (co)homology groups we maintain during the computation, such as their dimension. We provide both theoretical and experimental evidence of the efficiency of the method, and compare the implementation with state-of-the-art software for persistent (co)homology.
 
 
Clear and Compress: Computing Persistent Homology in Chunks
Michael Kerber, Max-Planck-Center for Visual Computing and Communication
 
Abstract: We present a parallelizable algorithm for computing the persistent homology of a filtered chain complex. Our approach differs from the commonly used reduction algorithm by first computing persistence pairs within local chunks, then simplifying the unpaired columns, and finally applying standard reduction on the simplified matrix. The approach generalizes a technique by G\"unther et al., which uses discrete Morse Theory to compute persistence; we derive the same worst-case complexity bound in a more general context. The algorithm employs several practical optimization techniques which are of independent interest. Our sequential implementation of the algorithm is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and we improve the performance through parallelized computation. Joint work with Ulrich Bauer and Jan Reininghaus.
 
 
Efficient computation of persistent homology for image data using discrete Morse theory
Hubert Wagner, Jagiellonian University
 
Abstract: We describe an efficient algorithm for computing persistent homology of 3D image data. Using Morse-Smale complex preprocessing together with an implicit representation of cubical complexes, memory usage is significantly reduced. This is an important improvement, as practical applications involve images containing at least 10^9 voxels. In such situations explicitly storing the boundary matrix of the input complex is prohibitive. We show that our method overcomes this difficulty, while being parallelizable and efficient in practical situations. 
 
Persistent Homology and Applications via Cellular Sheaves
Justin Curry, University of Pennsylvania
 
Abstract: One-dimensional persistent homology provides robust topological descriptors of data by sampling and aggregating over a parameter. Traditionally, one studies the sublevel sets of a parameter to get a sequence of vector spaces that are then depicted using "barcodes." Cellular sheaves provide an alternative way of organizing multi-dimensional persistence that is custom-tailored to the application at hand, thereby producing a compressed representation of the data. In this talk, I will show how to recover 1d-persistence and give examples in higher dimensions to illustrate the approach. If time permits, I will show how cellular sheaves adapt barcode-theoretic ideas to problems in network coding and sensor networks.
 
 
The persistence space as a descriptor in multidimensional persistence
Claudia Landi, Università di Modena e Reggio Emilia
 
Abstract: Multidimensional persistence is a tool for the topological analysis of data filtered by multiple parameters. An obstacle to the practical application of multidimensional persistence is that multidimensional persistence modules do not admit descriptors analogous to persistence diagrams. In this talk I will focus on multidimensional persistent Betti numbers, a.k.a. rank invariants, rather than on persistence modules. I will show that, in the functional setting, an analogue of the persistence diagram, called the persistence space, can be defined. It provides a complete descriptor of multidimensional persistent Betti numbers. In particular I will discuss the stability of persistence spaces.
 
 
Multidimensional Interleavings and Applications to Topological Inference
Michael Lesnick, Institute for Advanced Study
 
Abstract: Many of the most interesting theoretical results on persistent homology in the last few years have been formulated using a metric d_B on persistence modules called the bottleneck distance. To adapt these results to the setting of multidimensional persistent homology, we need a generalization of d_B to multidimensional persistence modules. Many generalizations of d_B to the multi-D setting are possible, and it is thus natural to ask whether there is in any sense, a unique "best" choice of a generalization of the bottleneck distance to the multi-D setting. I'll present results which give an answer to this question in the affirmative by showing that a generalization of the bottleneck distance called the interleaving distance satisfies a certain universal property, at least when we consider homology with coefficients in a prime field. I'll discuss consequences of this for the theory of multi-D topological inference and, if time permits, describe ongoing work in pursuit of a homotopy theoretic analogue of the universality result.
 
 
A Primer of Computational Homotopy Theory
Uli Wager, IST Austria 
 
Abstract: A basic question in algebraic topology is the study of maps between spaces up to homotopy (continuous deformation), and many important results and problems in topology can be seen as instances of the general problem of obtaining information about $[X,Y]$, the set of homotopy classes of maps between two spaces X and Y. In this talk, we survey some recent results, joint with Čadek, Krčál, Matoušek, Sergeraert, and Vokřínek (and building on earlier work by Sergeraert and his coworkers), on the computational complexity of a number of problems in homotopy theory. As we will see, for these problems there is often a fine line separating computability (or even polynomial-time algorithms) on the one hand from non-computability on the other hand.
